Activity 3: Geothermal Power Plant

Source of Energy

The geothermal power plant harnesses thermal energy from the Earth's interior, sourced from hot water or steam reservoirs beneath the surface. This renewable energy is available 24/7, making it highly reliable compared to intermittent sources like solar or wind.

Conversion Process

The conversion process involves drilling production wells to access geothermal reservoirs. Hot water or steam is extracted and directed to drive turbines. In dry steam plants, steam directly powers turbines; in flash steam systems, high-pressure hot water is flashed into steam. The turbine rotation generates electricity via connected generators.

Output/Utilization

The output is clean, baseload electricity that can be integrated into the grid. Spent geothermal fluid is often re-injected into the earth to maintain reservoir pressure and sustainability. A typical plant can generate 50-100 MW, sufficient for powering thousands of homes.

Real-World Application Relevance

In real-world applications, geothermal energy is prominent in volcanic regions like Iceland, where it provides over 25% of the country's electricity. The Geysers in California, the world's largest geothermal field, has been operational for decades, demonstrating long-term viability. This technology supports rural electrification and reduces fossil fuel dependence in suitable geological areas.

Overall, geothermal power plants offer a sustainable, low-emission energy solution with minimal land use, contributing significantly to global renewable energy goals while providing stable power for communities in geothermal-rich regions.
